The Game of Life is a simulation that creates beautiful life-like populations with very simple rules. Each node is either dead or alive, and each turn it asks itself 2 questions: Am I alive? Yes: How many living neighbors do I have? 2 or 3: STAY ALIVE not 2 or 3: DIE No: How many living neighbors do I have? 3: COME ALIVE not 3: STAY DEAD There are some wonderful natural patterns that pop up under these rules. Some groups are stable - they won't change unless bothered. Can you can find the smallest stable group? Can you find the largest stable group? Can you make a group that oscillates between 2 or more states, but returns to its starting value? EDIT: It's 2022 and I've recently returned to this project (which I made in 2017) to add some pizazz. Some new modifications allow you to control the parameters of the game: Birth conditions and Life conditions describe how many neighbors a cell must have to be born and to stay alive. The default for these two is 3 and 2,3 (respectively) but different conditions yield different population behaviors. The Neighborhood radius is how many nearby cells to check to satisfy the above conditions. Traditionally, the neighborhood of a cell is its 8 adjacent cells, but this parameter allows you to change the radius of that neighborhood. Spontaneity is the probability that a dead cell comes to life spontaneously, regardless of its neighboring conditions. This is not traditionally part of the Game of Life, but I thought it was a cool to see life form ex nihilo. Try something really small like 0.005, crank the speed up, and watch for life. The Save button will export a .txt file containing a list of lists of lists, that is [[[0,1],...],...] describing the history of all states that have been run since the last Clear. I have some code posted on github that can be viewed here. This code allows you to graph a game of life session using Sage Math. Finally, I've changed the topology so that the game is played on a torus rather than a square. This means that a Glider leaving out of the top will come back up from the bottom, rather than collapsing on a wall or disappearing. Your math enthusiast, Ben
